## The combined thesis

The distractor brainlift explains **how to infer the rule behind a wrong answer**. Robert White's competence brainlift explains **why a learner may voluntarily keep testing and refining rules** even without grades, prizes, fear, or pressure.

Together they suggest a much stronger product than “an exciting game that occasionally asks math questions”:

> **Build a world where every verified mathematical choice causes a coherent physical effect, every served counterfeit reveals the rule that caused it, and observed transfer gives the player new control over the world.**

The game should not primarily reward answers. It should reward effective interaction. The player's felt payoff is: **“I predicted what this system would do, changed it on purpose, and can now use it somewhere new.”**

### Product promise

**Learn the rule. Change the world.**

### Portfolio claim

**A specialized diagnostic SLM turns plausible student misconceptions into interactive world rules. The game uses those rules to create visible consequences, targeted repair, and evidence of transfer—not just correctness feedback.**

### Internal design name

Call the combined system the **Competence Engine**. This is not a player-facing psychological label. It is the product architecture connecting diagnostic evidence to challenge, agency, and world response. The physical `forecast → execute → inspect → manipulate → rerun` sequence inside an encounter is the **Effectance Encounter**.

## Why the two brainlifts fit unusually well

| Diagnostic-distractor idea | Competence/effectance idea | Game consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Student errors are systematic executable bugs | People enjoy learning the effects of their actions | Every served, verifier-approved counterfeit produces a stable, learnable machine behavior |
| A distractor matters only when its misconception is interpretable | Effective action, not passive reward, produces efficacy | The player sees and manipulates the causal error rather than reading “incorrect” |
| One answer cannot safely diagnose a learner | The feeling of efficacy can occur without real competence | The game never declares mastery after one correct choice; it gathers contrast and changed-context evidence |
| Registered wrong procedures must not reach the key | Apparent control can be illusory | Generation excludes keys reached by allowlisted bugs, then asks for a causal action to reduce guessing and unknown-strategy ambiguity |
| Formative information matters only when used to change the next action | Competence grows through repeated environment interaction | The selected misconception changes the very next repair and later world challenge |
| Error-focused strategy feedback helps; self-focused judgment harms | Strong pressure narrows attention and overrides exploration | Feedback addresses the machine's rule, never the child's ability; math pauses combat pressure |
| Good questions distinguish competing bugs | Interest comes from difference-in-sameness | Follow-ups preserve the skill but vary one feature to separate explanations |
| Misconceptions are topic-specific | Mostly familiar plus a small novelty supports engagement | The director changes one dimension at a time instead of jumping randomly across content |
| A procedure can be reconstructed and executed | Manipulation and construction are intrinsically motivating | The player repairs computations by rebuilding causal steps, not rereading explanations |

## The revised fiction

Glitches are not evil creatures and they are not random corruption. They are autonomous maintenance machines that learned a coherent but faulty mathematical rule. They execute that rule with impressive consistency. A Fraction Forger really does combine fraction parts the same wrong way every time; a Decimal Drifter reliably offsets place-value lanes; an Operation Swapper substitutes one system action for another.

This makes the machines a direct dramatization of Brown and Burton's core finding: a learner can be highly competent at following the wrong procedure.

The player is a **Field Debugger** driving the Aegis R4 rover through a damaged relay network. Their job is not to shoot mistakes away. Their job is to:

1. predict what a machine will do;
2. observe the actual effect;
3. infer the rule controlling it;
4. rebuild that rule;
5. recruit the repaired machine into the convoy;
6. test the rule later in a changed situation.

Once repaired, a Glitch becomes a useful companion module. The misconception is converted into a capability:

- Fraction Forger becomes the **Equalizer**, able to divide barriers and energy into equal-sized sections.
- Decimal Drifter becomes the **Aligner**, able to synchronize precision lanes and moving platforms.
- Operation Swapper becomes the **Sequencer**, able to select and lock the intended machine action.

The player's convoy is therefore not a trophy cabinet or mastery report. It is a visible set of machines they repaired and can now operate, with changed-context evidence tracked separately.

## New design pillars

### 1. Action must have a legible effect

Every important choice changes something visible, spatial, audible, and mechanically relevant. Selecting `4/12` does not merely tint a button red; it makes unequal physical segments try to lock together, shows the exact faulty computation, changes the enemy mechanism, and creates the repair opportunity.

### 2. Errors are hypotheses, not punishments

A wrong selection is useful evidence about one attempted rule. It reveals a coherent counterfactual and opens the richest part of the encounter. It does not remove lives, rewards, progress, or dignity.

### 3. Causal repair grants agency, not points

The primary reward is a new ability to manipulate the world after a valid causal strategy or repair action, regardless of help used: a repaired companion, route-control tool, construction behavior, or new system the player can operate. XP, stars, streaks, grades, and loot are not the motivational spine.

### 4. Calm cognition controls spectacular action

Driving and machine combat can be energetic. Mathematical decisions occur in tactical calm: time pressure stops, enemy aggression pauses, music opens space, and the player can inspect or manipulate without cost.

### 5. Understanding must survive a changed context

One correct answer produces an observation, not mastery. The free-use affordance is never withheld as an assessment reward; a later changed-context action is recorded separately, with no claim that one short session proves durable learning.

### 6. The player chooses their stimulation

The director offers a familiar route, a stretch route, and an exploratory route. It may recommend one but never hides the alternatives. The player can explicitly ask for steadier support or stranger challenges.

### 7. Leave room to tinker

Not every quiet moment gets filled by dialogue, markers, or a reward prompt. Repaired machines and world tools remain manipulable in optional workshop spaces with no score and no required outcome.

## The new core loop

### Phase 1: Choose an effect

An NPC presents a world problem, not a quiz command: restore a bridge, route battery charge, synchronize lift platforms, or divide coolant between systems. The player chooses which of several repair projects to pursue.

### Phase 2: Forecast

The game presents four neutral possible outcomes. These are the trusted answer and three SLM-authored, verified misconception outcomes. The prompt is “What will this configuration make happen?” rather than “Get the school question right.”

### Phase 3: Commit and observe

The player commits one route. The world performs it. A correct forecast produces the expected useful effect. A counterfeit produces its exact coherent malfunction. Either way, the action-effect relationship is visible.

### Phase 4: Trace the rule

The game replays the computation as a machine service diagram. It asks the player to identify the useful repair strategy before showing explanatory prose. This preserves active reconstruction.

### Phase 5: Counterbuild

The player repairs the rule by manipulating a small representation:

- resize fraction segments until their units match;
- drag decimal digits onto a shared place-value baseline;
- lock the story action to the correct operation;
- place negative values on a directional number line;
- order operation modules on a sequence rail.

Multiple mathematically valid strategies are accepted when possible. The goal is a stable result, not one prescribed hand motion.

After the server validates this causal action, the repaired machine joins the convoy and its portable tool becomes available. Support changes the evidence label, not the ability, spectacle, or campaign access.

### Phase 6: Contrast probe

If the first choice indicated a possible misconception, the deterministic engine creates a second question whose operands distinguish that bug from its nearest competing explanation. The SLM supplies verified distractors, including the target bug. The game phrases this as testing the repaired machine, not testing the child.

### Phase 7: Changed-context observation

Later—not immediately adjacent—the same underlying idea returns with fresh operands plus one meaningful context, representation, or operation-form change. A cue-free causal success is one within-session transfer observation; it is stronger than immediate repetition but not proof of durable understanding.

### Phase 8: Free-use and continue

The companion remains available during driving and exploration whether the changed-context attempt was independent, supported, or unsuccessful. A quiet route segment or workshop space lets the player use it voluntarily before the next contract.

## Three nested loops

### Moment loop: effectance

```text
act → perceive a specific effect → adjust → act more effectively
```

This loop must occur every few seconds during driving, manipulation, and repair. Responsive steering, physical circuit routing, and direct machine controls matter because competence motivation cannot be created by prose alone.

### Encounter loop: diagnosis

```text
forecast → outcome → candidate bug → repair → contrast probe
```

This is where the SLM is indispensable. It provides plausible alternative rules so the player's choice carries interpretable information.

### Campaign loop: autonomy

```text
choose project → gain capability → open new world interaction → choose a harder or stranger project
```

Progress is measured by the range of effects the player can intentionally create, not a numerical level.

## The competence evidence model

The game must distinguish **felt efficacy** from **demonstrated competence** without turning into a hidden high-stakes assessment. It must also keep two different questions separate:

1. **Misconception evidence:** did a particular counterfeit procedure recur on an item designed to distinguish it from rivals?
2. **Skill evidence:** can the player intentionally produce and explain the trusted effect, then do so later under changed conditions?

These cannot share one status field. A child can demonstrate a skill without ever choosing a wrong option, and a repaired wrong answer can show learning without proving that the original error was a stable misconception.

### Evidence types

1. **Forecast:** selected the correct outcome on a diagnostic item whose key cannot be reached by registered product procedures; this does not exclude guessing or an unknown faulty method.
2. **Discrimination:** distinguished the target misconception from a close confound on a contrast probe.
3. **Repair:** selected or constructed a strategy that fixes the causal step.
4. **Changed-context observation:** succeeded after one meaningful context or representation change.

### Misconception-hypothesis states

- `unseen`: no compatible wrong choice.
- `needs_check`: one selected counterfeit created a hypothesis.
- `check_deferred`: a supported/cued check could not resolve the hypothesis; wait for another activity before asking again.
- `repeated_pattern`: the same procedure recurred on a deliberately discriminating probe.
- `not_repeated`: the discriminating probe contradicted the initial hypothesis; retain the event without continuing to target it.
- `repairing`: the repeated procedure has an active supported repair.
- `resurfaced`: the procedure appeared again later and should receive another repair opportunity.

### Skill-evidence states

- `unseen`: no eligible action.
- `observed`: a trusted forecast or repair occurred, but could still reflect a guess or heavy support.
- `transfer_pending`: the player completed a causal strategy action, either by repairing a counterfeit or proving why a trusted route works.
- `transfer_observed`: a later independent, cue-free action succeeded with changed operands and context or representation. This is one within-session observation, not evidence of durable mastery.

Agency unlocks depend only on a server-validated causal strategy or repair action, never on making a mistake, avoiding help, or producing transfer evidence. The **skill track** controls only evidence claims and future practice. Correct and recovery routes therefore have equal functional progression, and a later error never confiscates an ability.

These states remain internal and evidence-specific. Player copy never says “You are a fraction expert” or “You have a decimal misconception.” It says what the player made happen:

- “You aligned two place-value systems without the guide.”
- “This repair held when the quantities changed.”
- “That route combined unequal pieces; the Equalizer can test a new configuration.”

### No permanent diagnosis from one response

A selected distractor creates a `needs_check` observation. The state becomes a repeated pattern only when a deliberately discriminating probe produces compatible evidence. A contradictory probe changes it to `not_repeated`; the original event remains in the session log but no longer drives targeted practice.

### Correct routes also require thinking

A correct numerical forecast can be a guess. Before it becomes skill evidence, the player performs a brief causal proof action—such as aligning the place-value rails, showing equivalent fraction cells, or tracing signed movement. This is a manipulation, not another MCQ. It keeps the correct route educationally equivalent to the recovery route without withholding its world payoff.

## The Challenge Envelope

The game should adapt two independent dimensions:

1. **Support:** how much representation, strategy scaffolding, and guided manipulation is available.
2. **Novelty:** how many aspects differ from demonstrated experience.

Do not collapse these into a single “difficulty” number.

### Novelty dimensions

- new operands with the same structure;
- new story surface with the same expression;
- new visual representation;
- new required strategy;
- added procedural step;
- related but distinct skill;
- combined familiar skills.

### Offer structure

At project forks, offer three understandable choices:

- **Steady route:** same idea, different values, full familiar tool access.
- **Stretch route:** satisfy the probe's required changes, then add exactly one extra novelty dimension.
- **Discovery route:** the same underlying learning target in an unfamiliar representation, with a safe introductory scaffold.

The player can also choose:

- “Give me a clearer model.”
- “Let me try without the guide.”
- “Show me a stranger version.”

These are better signals than silently inferring frustration from response time. Hesitation may reflect reading, accessibility needs, interruption, or careful thinking.

### Director rules

- After unsupported success, vary one novelty dimension.
- After supported success, keep novelty stable and reduce one support layer only if the player chooses.
- After a diagnostic wrong answer, keep the skill stable, expose the effect, and create a discriminating probe.
- After repeated unsuccessful repairs, leave combat staging, move to the quiet workshop, and provide a manipulable representation.
- After transfer success, offer a combined use or a new skill; do not force it.
- Never increase pressure, time limits, enemy damage, or reward stakes to make a hard cognitive task “motivating.”

## The Workshop: using “boredom” responsibly

White's evidence supports exploration and manipulation under low-pressure conditions. It does **not** establish that deliberately making a child bored will reliably improve learning. The product translation should therefore be **unfilled optional space**, not tedious waiting or withheld help.

The Workshop is a quiet, skippable area with:

- no quest marker until the player interacts or requests suggestions; an inactive player can remain uninterrupted for at least 20 seconds without being forced to wait;
- no timer, score, streak, or NPC reminder;
- repaired Glitches that react to direct manipulation;
- circuit blocks, fraction plates, number-line rails, and operation modules;
- world objects that invite “what happens if…” experimentation;
- a clearly available exit and a help control;
- optional construction goals that appear only after the player interacts.

Useful behavior is self-initiated testing, not time spent. If a learner immediately leaves, the game does not punish or nag them.

## Preventing fake competence

The competence brainlift correctly warns that the **feeling** of understanding is not proof of learning. Games are especially good at manufacturing that feeling through spectacle and easy wins.

### Design protections

- Never display “mastered” after one answer.
- Never let animation, position, color, or NPC tone reveal correctness before commitment.
- Construct items where registered wrong procedures cannot land on the correct answer.
- Require prediction before the system reveals its effect.
- Ask for repair action before explanatory text whenever the player has enough information.
- Separate immediate practice from later transfer.
- Recombine familiar Glitch tells in the boss rather than introducing an unlearned final gimmick.
- Make the optional reviewer report distinguish encounter, supported success, demonstrated repair, and transfer.
- Do not use session length, repeated clicks, or cosmetic collection as evidence of competence.

### Reconstruction mechanic

After selected encounters, present three service-diagram tiles:

1. the requested world action;
2. the rule the machine used;
3. the resulting physical effect.

The player reconstructs the causal chain. The game then asks which component must change. This is the game equivalent of rebuilding a brainlift's reasoning rather than rereading its conclusion.

## Revised combat and driving

### Combat

Keep spectacle but replace arbitrary hit points with **system stability**.

- A Glitch attacks by applying its faulty procedure to the environment.
- The player weakens it by predicting the result, exposing the causal trace, and choosing a valid counter-strategy.
- Driving skill positions the Aegis R4 at circuit junctions, but never substitutes for the mathematical repair.
- The Patch Cannon transmits a corrected rule; it does not injure a being.
- Defeat is a repair transformation: hostile panels align, lighting stabilizes, and the machine joins the convoy.

### Driving

Driving must also express competence:

- responsive acceleration, braking, and steering provide continuous action-effect feedback;
- repaired modules change traversal possibilities rather than merely raising stats;
- route choices expose different projects and novelty levels;
- no mandatory race timer during learning runs;
- optional time trials may exist later as a separate self-selected mode with no learning penalty.

### World abilities

- Equalizer: divide an energy beam or bridge into equal segments.
- Aligner: synchronize moving lanes, decimal rails, or precision docking points.
- Sequencer: order machine actions and lock a chosen operation.
- Polarity Anchor: reverse directional fields and stabilize negative-number zones.
- Factor Mesh: group or tile structures into common arrays.

Only abilities supported by the SLM's validated Number scope enter the first campaign.

## NPC redesign

NPCs provide goals, constraints, and expertise; they do not behave like teachers holding quiz cards.

### Nia — systems engineer

- Names the desired world effect and asks what the player notices.
- Gives exact task-focused observations without announcing a diagnosis.
- Asks “What do you want this relay to do?” rather than “What is the right answer?”
- Offers the trace only on request or after the player has first inspected the effect.
- Withdraws guidance when the player requests independent control.

### Route scanner — challenge choice

- Offers steady, stretch, and discovery routes with honest previews.
- Shows which dimension will change without displaying a hidden ability rating.
- Treats exploration as useful mapping, not deviation from the lesson.
- Gives all three routes equal campaign progress.

### Repaired test drone — mobile workbench

- Stores previously observed rules as rerunnable machine protocols.
- Lets the player change bounded inputs and predict the result.
- Provides quiet experimentation without generated chatter or a completion bar.
- Never labels the player or summarizes a permanent weakness.

### Dialogue rules

- Describe task, action, evidence, or next strategy.
- Never praise intelligence, label ability, or diagnose identity.
- Avoid generic “Great job!” as the primary feedback.
- Prefer “That alignment held when the values changed.”
- Offer choices without pretending every path is equivalent.
- Use silence after an invitation; do not fill every pause with chatter.

## Exact roles of each intelligence layer

### Deterministic code owns

- skill graph and supported misconception registry;
- operands, expression, correct answer, and trusted solution;
- false-positive-key rejection;
- bug-output collision analysis;
- discriminating contrast-probe construction;
- computation evaluation and grounding;
- evidence updates and agency unlock requirements;
- option neutrality, safety, and final gameplay authority.

### The diagnostic SLM owns

- proposing three plausible diagnostic counterfeits for the verified question;
- naming the student-like procedure behind each proposal;
- showing a question-specific computation;
- proposing complete distractor sets that the verifier may accept for a requested target when that misconception happens to appear uniquely; this is rejection-based serving control, not yet demonstrated model steerability.

The SLM does not decide truth, learner identity, reward, difficulty, or mastery.

### Sonnet owns

- bounded NPC/world story skins around fixed mathematics;
- bounded tone selection around canonical feedback facts;
- optional variation of service-diagram wording;

Sonnet does not invent or paraphrase unchecked mathematics, receive the session evidence log, diagnose from raw behavior, decide which evidence counts, or write the reviewer report. Deterministic code renders the mathematical explanation and adult-facing evidence summary; Sonnet may make the surrounding character line feel natural.

This is an explicit narrowing of the tempting “let Sonnet generate the adaptive math” idea. The live experience is still custom: deterministic code selects and compiles the exact misconception-separating probe, Sonnet skins that fixed structure as a current world problem without receiving operands or answer history, and the SLM proposes its counterfeits. Letting Sonnet author or freely paraphrase the mathematical correction would create a second unverified math model and weaken the portfolio claim that the specialized SLM plus exact gate owns the diagnostic work.

### The 3D client owns

- legible physical cause and effect;
- manipulation, driving, camera, animation, audio, and accessibility;
- neutral options before commitment;
- presenting player choices and support controls;
- never inferring mathematical state from generated prose.

## Product modes

### 1. Story Run

An 8–12 minute portfolio slice: choose one Fraction Forger project, observe it across primary/contrast/changed-context encounters, repair it, recruit it as a companion, and face a short diagnostic-convoy phase.

### 2. Workshop

Quiet self-directed manipulation with repaired machines. No score, timer, required sequence, or generated dialogue loop.

### 3. Behind the Forge

Reviewer mode showing:

- verified question blueprint;
- owner-only, escaped raw SLM proposal versus canonicalized gameplay output; this surface is excluded from child builds and never contains learner identifiers or Sonnet secrets;
- deterministic rejection reasons;
- selected misconception evidence;
- why the contrast probe distinguishes competing bugs;
- which causal action unlocked agency, shown separately from any changed-context evidence.

### 4. Reviewed Learning

Future child-ready mode using only owner-reviewed or deterministically canonicalized bundles, local/ephemeral evidence, bounded content, and approved provider retention.

### 5. Educator Field Report — later product

Report actions rather than scores:

- “Direct numerator-and-denominator addition was selected twice on discriminating items in this session.”
- “An equal-sized-piece repair was followed by one successful changed-context battery item.”
- “Next useful action: compare unlike denominators with a fraction-strip model.”

Do not build a broad teacher dashboard into the first slice. Prove the evidence report with one session first, and never translate one session into a durable individual diagnosis.

## Vertical-slice content

The first competence-integrated slice should include:

1. one Fraction Forger contract;
2. one fraction contrast probe that separates two plausible procedures;
3. one direct fraction-lattice repair;
4. one delayed fraction transfer challenge in a changed world context;
5. one Equalizer companion ability used during driving;
6. one quiet Workshop interval;
7. one diagnostic-convoy phase chosen from observed session protocols;
8. one Behind the Forge replay that can show the SLM's broader verified Number-scope benchmark without claiming those world interactions are complete.

This is enough to demonstrate the combined product mechanism. Adding more districts, Glitches, skills, or progression before these eight pieces work would hide the research contribution under content volume.

After this loop passes its causal-clarity and safety gates, decimal rails and integer polarity become separate expansion plans. Their schemas and art sockets may be reserved now, but their gameplay should not compete with the first polished manipulation for implementation time.

## Success measures

### Diagnostic integrity

- percentage of served options with unique executable misconception mappings;
- false-positive-key rejection rate;
- contrast probes that separate the target bug from its nearest confound;
- targeted follow-up acceptance yield;
- consistency between selected option, revealed machine behavior, and feedback.

### Within-session learning evidence

- repair strategy success after an observed malfunction;
- delayed changed-context success with fresh operands plus one meaningful surface/representation change;
- ability to predict a repaired Glitch's effect;
- contradictions that correctly reduce a candidate misconception rather than cementing a label.

### Effectance and autonomy

- players report understanding what their actions changed;
- voluntary use of a new companion ability;
- self-selected steady/stretch/discovery routes;
- voluntary Workshop experiments;
- requests for more or less support;
- re-engagement after a wrong forecast without coercive reward.

Do not optimize time-on-task, streak length, daily return, click volume, or reward collection as proxies for learning or competence.

---

## What not to build

- a normal quiz with combat animations after each answer;
- XP awarded for correctness;
- daily streaks, lives, energy, loot, or escalating loss pressure;
- a difficulty system that mistakes speed for understanding;
- an NPC that overpraises every action;
- automatic “mastery” after one correct response;
- a permanent learner label derived from one distractor;
- forced idle time presented as “productive boredom”;
- open-ended child chat with Sonnet;
- an open world before the Competence Engine works in one compact district;
- a teacher dashboard before the evidence is valid and actionable.

## The sharper game pitch

> **Mathbreakers: Counterfeit Protocol is a 3D diagnostic rover adventure where verified wrong options become machines with consistent rules. Players forecast what the machines will do, observe the consequences, and rebuild the faulty logic. Repaired Glitches become world-changing tools for every player; later probes record whether the rule also holds in a changed situation. A specialized 4B model creates the diagnostic counterfeits, and exact code verifies and adapts them.**

That combination is more original and more defensible than “AI-generated math questions inside a racing game.” It makes both brainlifts structural: the SLM supplies interpretable bugs, and competence motivation supplies the reason the player keeps acting on them.
